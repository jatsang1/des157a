Log 2: Overlay Design

The article "Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows" by Naema Baskanderi discusses overlays and how to use them appropriately. This article helped me in putting a name to a design pattern I'd seen all over the internet. It was intriguing to learn that it has several names. Personally, I find this design pattern to be annoying at times, but I believe this is dependent on how the site designer employs it. One of the preventive measures offered by Baskanderi that I found very appealing was the user-initiated overlay. This is because I despise it when I'm going through a website and a popup window appears. It's occurred so many times that I've learned to ignore overlays when they appear. However, I believe the design pattern has the potential to be effective if designers examine its advantages and regulate how it disrupts user flow.

Log 1: Form Design

Completing a form isn't always the most enjoyable experience. At times, it can feel tedious or difficult to finish. Some people might find themselves abandoning a form, or switching to another task. As designers, it is important to take into consideration the usability of our forms because it affects the accessibility, attention, enjoyment, and success of them. The article "Best practices for form design" by Salim Ansari shares helpful protocols/tips to address this. Some examples that stood out to me was providing a clear visual of progression or how many steps are left as way to motivate form completion, and reduce the user's cognitive burden and visual noise by dividing a long form into seperate sections/steps.

One website that I've interacted with that exemplifies some of these practices is Duolingo. They chunk their surveys, courses and language learning into a singular step-by-step process, and they provide a progress bar to let their users know the level of completion. By having these practices in place, the user is not overwhelmed by a load of information and is encouraged to stay on task.
